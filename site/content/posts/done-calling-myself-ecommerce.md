For most of my career, I've introduced myself as someone who works in digital commerce. Ecommerce. Digital marketing. Customer experience in retail and luxury. It's an accurate description of where I've spent my time - Estée Lauder, Shiseido, Microsoft, and brands across Asia-Pacific and beyond.

But somewhere in the last few years, I noticed something: the most interesting problems I was being asked to solve weren't really ecommerce problems. They were *change* problems. Technology adoption problems. Leadership alignment problems. Problems that look the same whether the organization sells skincare, financial services, or industrial equipment.

I've started calling them what they actually are: enterprise transformation challenges.

## The Industry Trap

There's a comfortable story most professionals tell about themselves: "I'm a retail person." "I'm a fintech person." "I'm deep in logistics." The industry label signals expertise, reassures clients, and makes you easier to slot into an org chart.

The problem is that it also narrows the aperture of what you're allowed to be useful for.

I've spent two decades helping organizations figure out how to change - how to move from legacy tech to modern platforms, from gut-driven decisions to data-informed ones, from fragmented customer journeys to coherent experiences. That's not an ecommerce skill set. That's a transformation skill set that *happened to play out* in ecommerce.

The sector was the context. The work was always about change.

## What the Switch Actually Looks Like

Making a move into broader territory at this stage of a career isn't about abandoning what you know. It's about recognising that what you know is more transferable than the label you've been using suggests.

A few things I've learned from making this shift:

**Your war stories travel well.** The specifics of a Shopify migration or a loyalty program rebuild might not resonate in a healthcare context. The story of why an executive team resisted a critical data initiative - and how you navigated it - resonates everywhere. Lead with the pattern, not the platform.

**The "industry expertise" objection is often a proxy for something else.** When a new sector client says "we need someone with our industry experience," they usually mean one of two things: either they're worried you won't understand their regulatory environment, or they've been burned by consultants who brought generic frameworks with no context. Address those directly. Show you've done the homework on their sector. Show your frameworks are built from real delivery, not theory.

**Fractional and advisory roles lower the barrier for both sides.** A CFO in professional services isn't going to hand you a $2M transformation program on your first engagement outside your vertical. But they might ask you to spend two days helping shape their digital roadmap. Do that well, and the rest follows. Most career pivots happen one project at a time, not in a single leap.

## The Age Question

There's an honest conversation to be had about timing. Making a significant career pivot at mid-40s carries a different weight than doing it at 30.

On the one hand, your credibility is real. You've been through enough cycles - technology hype, organizational politics, failed implementations - to know what actually matters. That's worth something. A lot of organizations are tired of hiring people who've read about transformation. They want someone who's *done* it.

On the other hand, there's an energy question. Genuine pivots require investment: building new networks, writing for a new audience, being the learner again in rooms where you used to be the expert. That's uncomfortable at any age. At 45, you have less runway and more obligations. The pivot needs to be intentional, not reactive.

The practical answer is to move gradually. Broaden the framing of your existing work before you try to step into an entirely new sector. Write about the cross-industry patterns you're seeing. Take the adjacent engagements that stretch you. Let the positioning shift precede the role change.
